Tasks Today's tasks centered on adding server-side persistence to a Tic-Tac-Toe. Admittedly, solving this has been something I have already done with engineering experience, so I did not take it as seriously as I should have because it fit well within my comfort range.

Much of today's tasks were introductory:

  • Learning and understanding basic CSS
  • A brief dive into TailwindCSS

Technical Lessons

  • Learning about costs of passing too much state around.
    • For example, if board state is persisted on the client, that lends itself vulnerable to attack (ex. a rogue Google Chrome extension), or manipulation.
    • Instead, storing the data on the server both minimizes the request payload (which helps with scaling to millions/billions of requests and save money) and deters malicious actors.
    • Only store the minimum amount of state you need passed around. In this case, all the client needs is to send the position to the server

Non-Technical Lessons

  • I spoke with Jon, a fellow classmate and doctor. I shared some of my old work on Kasa before. He suggested an interesting approach - just reach out and ask companies for the internship directly.

  • The Fractal program requires us to do "internships" with companies, some of which are paid, some of which aren't.

  • In my head, "internship" was a term that only applied to college students. I had to realize I was too constrained in my thinking about the word "internship," and I could instead use the relevant work I had on Kasa to reach out to health tech companies directly. The idea is: "I've already worked on some of my own health tech software - here is how I could do the same or similar in a short internship for you."

  • It's so simple, but effective. Fractal usually sets up students with internships. But why wait for an internship when I can just advocate for myself? Will need to update on this.

Career Positioning

  • I spent a considerable amount of time speaking with Andrew about positioning myself. Prior to Fractal, I spent a considerable amount of time building Kasa . Not only was my goal to work on something substantive, but to reduce the friction from when I finished Fractal to when I started hunted for an engineering role at a larger company. I don't want to make a portfolio piece, I want to lead with "real work" and demonstrate tangible value when pitching myself.

  • Andrew first wondered if I should focus on Kasa instead, rather than some of the introductory assignments if I wasn't getting the most value out of them. This is really important to consider as the program builds around your strengths rather than you following a standardized or traditional path. In Fractal, you set your own personal asymptote.

  • I expressed that my goal was not to work on Kasa super long-term, but mainly put the best effort into this right now to prepare me to land the next role.

  • Ultimately, Andrew came to the conclusion that he thinks I am able to get the interview, so I should put all of my energy on becoming more hireable rather than improving Kasa to become more functional than it already. If it does the job in highlighting my strengths / decision-making enough, even if's not the most advanced, I need to focus on the biggest priority for me career wise, which is working.

  • He advised I should directly find the type of role I want, and use Claude to work backwards on how to achieve it. Andrew drove the point home that he believed I had the capacity to get to these kinds of roles in <7 weeks (which I'm not so sure about), or make 150-200k. My incredulity probably speaks to a larger lack of confidence. To this end, my reservation was that even if I could get the interview, I was not yet prepared to meet the bar in terms of the Leetcode / system design round. This reservation has held me back.

  • With Andrew's encouragement, he affirmed that I should focus on finding the places where I would want to work, feel I am contributing meaningfully, and feel I could create an outsized impact for a company and their mission. All these take precedent before worrying about not being able to meet the interviewing bar. I've never thought of removing the interview bar from my mind - if you can't pass the bar, who cares if you identify with the mission or are a great fit? I will try this new approach.

Start with identifying the confidence in your capacities, and then work to identify the skills you need to backfill.
